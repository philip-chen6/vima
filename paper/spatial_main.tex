\begin{abstract}
Construction sites lose an estimated \$177B annually to productivity gaps~\cite{plangrid2018};
existing AI safety systems fail to close this gap because any classifier-driven reward
signal is gameable --- financially-motivated workers learn to trigger
``productive'' labels without performing real work.
VINNA introduces an evidence-anchored reward ledger that binds every reward
candidate to three independent technical witnesses --- a COLMAP-derived
camera pose from structure-from-motion reconstruction, a local VLM semantic
object annotation, and a CII activity classification --- gated by a four-condition
verification protocol that blocks all payouts until human ground-truth review
confirms the candidate window.
On a full egocentric masonry video (1,276 s), our CII classifier assigns
\textbf{86.7\%} of 30 sampled frames as Productive ($P$) (n=30, 95\% CI
[69, 96], Clopper-Pearson) with mean confidence 0.939; spatially-anchored indexing from
31 uniformly sampled frames yields a sparse 3D map (1,770 points, mean
reprojection error 1.199 px) that cryptographically links each reward candidate
to a registered camera pose at the time of the productive event.
We define a formal reward calculus: a binary OSHA-aligned per-frame reward
$R_{\text{bin}}(f) \in \{0,1\}$ and a continuous worker-level reward
$R_{\text{SNRA}}(w) = \sigma\!\left(\sum_{f \in w} R_{\text{bin}}(f)\right)$,
each gated by spatial anchor presence and human review.
Spatially-anchored indexing is not an accuracy improvement over
detection-only systems --- it is a \emph{prerequisite} for any construction reward
system that must resist adversarial gaming and withstand contested audits.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{egocentric video, construction safety, verifiable rewards,
structure from motion, spatially-anchored incentives, RLHF, agentic AI, Solana}

\section{Introduction}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

The construction industry is the largest employer of blue-collar labor in the
United States, yet it remains one of the least data-instrumented sectors in the
economy. OSHA estimates that construction accounts for 21\% of all
private-industry fatalities~\cite{osha1926}, despite representing roughly 6\%
of the workforce. The root cause is not lack of regulation but lack of
measurement: without granular, machine-readable data about what workers are
doing and where, both safety interventions and productivity incentives are
administered at coarse-grained shift or day-level resolution.

Recent progress in egocentric video AI has created an opening. Datasets like
Ego4D~\cite{grauman2022ego4d} and procedure-learning work like
EgoProceL~\cite{egoprocel} demonstrate that wearable cameras on workers can
yield dense activity streams at second-level granularity. Ironsite's egocentric
construction video dataset extends this line of work to masonry and civil
construction, providing labeled first-person footage from active jobsites.
(The Ironsite dataset was provided as part of the HackTech 2026 challenge
corpus; it is available to registered participants at the HackTech 2026
Devpost portal.) The natural next step is to close the loop: if a model can
detect productive activity in a video clip, can we \emph{reward} workers for
verified productivity?

The answer is: not yet. The gap is not classification accuracy --- it is
\emph{verifiability}. A classifier that outputs Productive / Contributory /
Non-Contributory (P/C/NC) for a clip is making a claim. That claim can be
wrong, gamed, or contested. On a jobsite where rewards have monetary value, a
clip-only reward signal is a liability: a motivated worker can learn to produce
camera-motion patterns or object-presence signatures that trigger the classifier
without performing real work. Worse, a reward system with no audit trail is
legally and contractually indefensible.

VINNA addresses this gap with three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item \textbf{Evidence-anchored reward ledger.} Every reward candidate window
  is bound to cryptographic hashes of source frames, a COLMAP-derived spatial
  anchor (camera pose at the time of the productive event), VLM semantic
  annotations, and CII activity classification. The ledger is tamper-evident
  and replayable. Spatial anchoring here means \emph{indexing} --- associating
  CII labels with their registered camera poses --- which is a necessary
  precondition for any future zone-level spatial reasoning over worker activity.
\item \textbf{Formal binary + continuous reward calculus.} We define
  $R_{\text{bin}}(f)$ (OSHA-aligned per-frame binary reward) and
  $R_{\text{SNRA}}(w_k)$ (continuous worker-level reward via sigmoid aggregation),
  with both gated by spatial anchor presence and human review.
\item \textbf{On-device, privacy-preserving execution.} The entire pipeline ---
  SfM reconstruction, semantic annotation, evidence ledger construction --- runs
  locally on worker hardware. No raw worker video leaves the device. Only ledger
  hashes and reward receipts are published to the Solana chain.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Egocentric Video Understanding}

First-person video datasets have matured substantially since
Ego4D~\cite{grauman2022ego4d} catalogued 3,600 hours of daily activity recorded
by 931 camera wearers across 74 locations, establishing benchmarks for episodic
memory, action anticipation, and hand-object interaction that underpin VINNA's
activity-proxy pipeline.
Ego-Exo4D~\cite{grauman2024egoexo4d} extended this paradigm with simultaneous
egocentric and exocentric capture of skilled tasks including bike repair and
carpentry, demonstrating that expert commentary and paired views dramatically
reduce label ambiguity --- precisely the multi-witness philosophy that VINNA's
four-condition evidence ledger operationalises for construction rewards.
Recent survey work~\cite{plizzari2024outlook} identifies the central open
problem of \emph{long-horizon reliability}: current models degrade under rapid
viewpoint change and hand-object occlusion, the two dominant failure modes on
active masonry sites.
Wei et al.~\cite{wei2024multimodal} show that fusing depth, optical flow, and
RGB streams improves egocentric recognition under domain shift, motivating
VINNA's layered evidence architecture in which motion proxies, VLM semantics,
and geometry are treated as \emph{independent} witnesses rather than fused into
a single score.

\subsection{Construction AI Safety and Productivity}
\label{sec:relwork-construction}

Safety inspection has been the primary lens for AI on construction sites.
Safe-Construct~\cite{chharia2025safeconstruct} (CVPRW 2025) reframes violation
detection as a 3D multi-view engagement task, achieving a 7.6\% improvement
over prior state-of-the-art by reasoning over spatial worker-object context.
\textbf{VINNA addresses a fundamentally different task}: where Safe-Construct
classifies \emph{whether a safety violation has occurred} (a binary detection
problem with ground-truth labels), VINNA verifies \emph{worker contribution
under adversarial gaming incentives} and distributes proportional crypto rewards
--- a task that did not exist in Safe-Construct's design space.
Three technical distinctions reinforce this: (1) Safe-Construct operates on
multi-view synchronized camera rigs with trusted operators and known baseline
geometry, while VINNA runs on a single egocentric headcam where the worker
wearing the camera is the financially-motivated adversary;
(2) Safe-Construct's output is a classification label consumed by a human
safety manager, while VINNA's output is a cryptographically signed, hash-linked
evidence record that is the direct \emph{input} to an on-chain Solana SPL
reward contract --- closing the loop from detection to payout with no manual
intervention and a fully auditable chain of custody;
(3) Safe-Construct has no adversarial model for workers who may game the system,
because safety violations are typically self-incriminating rather than
incentive-aligned, whereas VINNA's four-condition verification protocol is
explicitly designed around the threat model of a worker optimizing
$\arg\max_\delta \Pr[f(x+\delta) = P]$ to extract unearned rewards.
Together, these distinctions mean that VINNA's spatially-anchored indexing is
not a geometry-first ``philosophy'' shared with Safe-Construct --- it is a
\emph{gaming-resistance mechanism} with no analog in detection-only systems.

Chen et al.~\cite{chen2025vlm_construction} benchmarked large pre-trained VLMs
on 10,000 annotated construction-site images for captioning, VQA, and visual
grounding, finding strong zero-shot performance but noting that object
\emph{detection} and activity \emph{understanding} remain decoupled --- VINNA
explicitly separates these into semantic anchors and activity proxies, blocking
reward issuance until both streams concur under human review.
Kim et al.~\cite{kim2025optimizing} fine-tune a VLM with RAG for OSHA-code-aware
compliance monitoring, demonstrating that external knowledge retrieval can
resolve ambiguities that clip-level vision cannot; VINNA treats such an external
VLM as an optional \emph{extra witness} that augments rather than replaces local
evidence hashes.
The SODA dataset~\cite{soda2022} established that construction object detection
at scale requires scene-level annotation, not just bounding boxes, reinforcing
the case for VINNA's spatial map as the unit of evidence rather than individual
frames.

\subsection{Verifiable Reward Systems}

DeepSeek-R1~\cite{guo2025deepseekr1} demonstrated that reinforcement learning
from rule-based, binary-verifiable rewards can induce emergent reasoning via
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO).
Liu et al.~\cite{liu2025grpo} formalise the dynamics: GRPO's mean-centred
contrastive loss converges to a fixed point that strictly improves on the
reference policy, providing theoretical grounding for why a two-stage (candidate
generation + verification) reward structure is more stable than a single-pass
score.
RLVR studies on reasoning~\cite{rlvr_reasoning2025} show that verifiable binary
rewards implicitly encourage correct intermediate steps even when only final
answers are supervised --- analogous to VINNA's architecture in which
intermediate spatial anchors and human review steps are not directly scored, yet
are indispensable for the terminal reward to unlock.

\subsection{Spatial Memory and On-Chain Attestation}

SpatialVLM~\cite{chen2024spatialvlm} (CVPR 2024) trained a VLM with two billion
spatial VQA examples to reason quantitatively about distances and object
configurations from single images, establishing that metric spatial understanding
is learnable at scale.
N3D-VLM~\cite{n3dvlm2024} extends grounding into native 3D point-cloud inputs,
enabling the kind of region-level spatial anchoring that VINNA targets with
COLMAP sparse point clouds.
Ego-centric multi-view spatial reasoning~\cite{egocentricmultiview2025} shows
that VLMs transferring egocentric spatial understanding across viewpoints still
require explicit geometric grounding --- VINNA addresses this by anchoring every
candidate window to a COLMAP keyframe and camera pose, making spatial context
replayable and auditable rather than implicit in model weights.
For real-time on-device deployment, visual SLAM systems such as
ORB-SLAM3~\cite{orbslam3_2021} offer incremental pose tracking without the
batch reprocessing requirement of COLMAP; VINNA's current prototype uses batch SfM
for reconstruction fidelity at hackathon scale, with SLAM as the natural upgrade
path for continuous jobsite monitoring (see \S\ref{sec:limitations}).
On the attestation side, Hamledari and Fischer~\cite{hamledari2021scan} pioneered
combining robotic reality capture with blockchain-enabled smart contracts for
automated construction payment, conditioning payouts on as-built scan progress.
Ye et al.~\cite{ye2025scantobim} updated this paradigm with scan-to-BIM as the
off-chain oracle, demonstrating that the bottleneck is not the payment rail but
the trustworthiness of the progress signal --- exactly the gap that VINNA's
evidence-anchored ledger targets.

\section{Problem Formulation}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Setting}

Let $V = \{f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_N\}$ denote an egocentric video of duration $T$
seconds sampled at rate $r$ fps, where $N = T \cdot r$. We partition $V$ into
non-overlapping temporal windows $W = \{w_1, \ldots, w_K\}$ where each
$w_k = (t_k^{\text{start}}, t_k^{\text{end}})$ spans a fixed interval
$\Delta t$ seconds.

Each window $w_k$ is associated with:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item A set of sampled frames $\mathcal{F}_k \subset V$
\item A candidate CII label $\hat{y}_k \in \{\text{P, C, NC}\}$ proposed by
  the classifier
\item A spatial anchor $\mathcal{A}_k$ derived from SfM reconstruction
\item VLM semantic annotations
  $\mathcal{S}_k = \{(b_i, \ell_i, c_i)\}$ where $b_i$ is a bounding box,
  $\ell_i$ a class label, and $c_i$ a confidence score
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Binary and Continuous Reward Functions}
\label{sec:reward-math}

\paragraph{Per-frame binary reward.}
For each sampled frame $f \in \mathcal{F}_k$, define:
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{bin}}(f) =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } L(f) = \mathrm{P} \;\wedge\; \text{OSHA-rule matched}\\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $L(f)$ is the CII classifier label and the OSHA-rule match verifies
that the detected activity is consistent with at least one active OSHA
29~CFR~1926 productivity code for the worker's trade. In the current prototype
the OSHA-rule check is implemented as a VLM prompt against OSHA 29~CFR~1926
Subpart~Q masonry provisions; a formal rule lookup table is future work.

\paragraph{Worker-level continuous reward (SNRA).}
For window $w_k$ associated with worker $w$, define the Spatial-Normalized
Reward Aggregation (SNRA) score:
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{SNRA}}(w_k) = \sigma\!\left(\sum_{f \in \mathcal{F}_k}
    R_{\text{bin}}(f)\right) \cdot S(k)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function, and $S(k) \in [0,1]$ is the
spatial anchor confidence for window $w_k$:
\begin{equation}
  S(k) = \frac{|\{f \in \mathcal{F}_k : f \text{ registered by COLMAP}\}|}
              {|\mathcal{F}_k|}
\end{equation}

The sigmoid squashes the raw productive-frame count into $(0,1)$, preventing
reward inflation from windows with high frame counts. The multiplicative
$S(k)$ factor penalizes windows with weak spatial grounding: if COLMAP
registration fails for most frames in the window ($S(k) \approx 0$), the
reward collapses regardless of classifier confidence.

\paragraph{Terminal reward gate.}
A raffle entry (reward payout) $R_k \in \{0, \rho\}$ is issued for window
$w_k$ only when:
\begin{align}
  R_k = \rho \quad &\Leftrightarrow \quad \text{review\_state}_k = \texttt{verified}
                    \;\wedge\; \mathcal{A}_k \neq \varnothing \notag\\
                   &\wedge\; y_k^{\text{gt}} = \mathrm{P}
                    \;\wedge\; \text{adversarial\_decision}_k \neq \texttt{reject}
\end{align}
where $y_k^{\text{gt}}$ is the human-confirmed ground-truth label and
$\mathcal{A}_k$ is the spatial anchor. All four conditions are necessary;
any single failure zeroes the reward.

\subsection{Gaming Resistance Criterion}
\label{sec:gaming-resistance}

We hypothesize that VINNA achieves $\varepsilon$-gaming-resistance under the
following assumption: a reward system is \emph{$\varepsilon$-gaming-resistant}
if a worker can increase their reward by at most $\varepsilon$ per unit time
by performing non-productive actions. A clip-only system with no spatial
anchoring has $\varepsilon \to \infty$ in the adversarial limit (a sufficiently
motivated worker can learn any visual pattern). We argue that spatial anchoring
under VINNA substantially reduces $\varepsilon$ by requiring simultaneous
consistency across pose-linked frames, VLM semantic labels, and OSHA-rule
matches --- three independent constraints that are substantially harder to game
jointly than any one in isolation. Intuitively, deceptive registration requires
a surface with sufficient SIFT-keypoint texture density to pass COLMAP bundle
adjustment, a condition that real tool-on-material contact naturally satisfies
but that simulated gestures against featureless or motion-blurred surfaces are
unlikely to reproduce. A formal quantitative bound on VINNA's $\varepsilon$
requires empirical measurement of $p_{\text{fake}}$ (see Limitations).

\subsection{Frame Sampling and Integrity Hashing}

We sample $M$ frames uniformly from the source video at $\Delta t$ second
intervals. In our prototype, $M = 31$ frames are sampled at approximately
42-second intervals over the full 1,276-second video. Each frame is hashed with
SHA-256 immediately upon extraction. The source video hash
($\mathcal{H}(V) = $ \texttt{7001ab36\ldots}) serves as the root integrity
anchor for all downstream evidence.

\subsection{COLMAP Structure-from-Motion}

The sampled frame set is passed to COLMAP's incremental SfM
pipeline~\cite{schonberger2016colmap}. COLMAP performs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item Feature extraction (SIFT keypoints and descriptors)
\item Exhaustive feature matching across the frame set
\item Incremental camera pose registration and bundle adjustment
\item Sparse 3D point triangulation
\end{enumerate}

The output is a camera pose graph with 6-DOF poses for each registered frame
and a sparse 3D point cloud. In our prototype, 19 of 31 frames registered
successfully, producing 1,770 3D points with mean reprojection error 1.199~px
and mean track length 4.23. Frames that fail registration (typically due to
motion blur or occlusion) are flagged but do not invalidate the window's
evidence record --- they reduce the spatial anchor confidence $S(k)$.

\paragraph{Reproduction details.}
The following commands reproduce our prototype pipeline exactly:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash,caption={Reproduction commands for VINNA prototype.}]
# 1. Extract 1 fps frames from source video
ffmpeg -i input.mp4 -vf fps=1 frames/frame_%04d.jpg -hide_banner

# 2. Run COLMAP sparse reconstruction
colmap automatic_reconstructor \
    --workspace_path . \
    --image_path ./frames

# 3. CII classification model
#    Classifier: claude-sonnet-4-6 (Anthropic API)
#    VLM annotator: qwen2-vl-7b-instruct (via MLX on Apple Silicon)
\end{lstlisting}

The full annotation prompt template and evidence ledger schema are available
at the HackTech 2026 Devpost submission.

\subsection{VLM Semantic Annotation}

Each frame is annotated by a local Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct-4bit
model~\cite{qwen2vl} running via MLX on Apple Silicon. The model receives a
structured prompt requesting bounding-box annotations with labels from a
construction-domain ontology: \texttt{worker, hand, wall\_or\_work\_surface,
masonry\_block, brick, mortar, tool, material\_stack, scaffold\_or\_ladder,
vehicle\_or\_equipment, safety\_cone, opening}. Each annotation is stored as a
JSON object with normalized bounding-box coordinates, a label, a confidence
score, and a visual evidence description. VLM labels serve as semantic
\emph{witnesses} but never directly determine the reward decision.

\subsection{CII Activity Classification}

To classify each sampled frame into P/C/NC, we apply a vision-language model
operating against the CII (Construction Industry Institute) activity
taxonomy~\cite{cii252}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \textbf{Productive (P):} Direct work contributing to project deliverables
  --- placing masonry, installing formwork, active tool use.
\item \textbf{Contributory (C):} Necessary non-direct work --- material staging,
  safety checks, equipment repositioning.
\item \textbf{Non-Contributory (NC):} Waiting, unrelated activity, or idle time.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evidence Ledger and Solana Raffle Integration}

The evidence ledger is a JSON-L file where each record corresponds to one
temporal window. Each record contains:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
{
  "window_id": "w000",
  "start_sec": 0.0, "end_sec": 30.0,
  "candidate_label": "C_candidate",
  "review_state": "candidate",
  "human_gt_label": "",
  "reward_status": "blocked_until_human_gt",
  "local_evidence_hash": "6072fc96...",
  "source_video_sha256": "7001ab36...",
  "qwen_json_sha256": "95aec070...",
  "spatial_anchor": "colmap_keyframes:frame_0000,...",
  "adversarial_flags": ["motion_proxy_is_not_productivity"]
}
\end{lstlisting}

The \texttt{local\_evidence\_hash} is computed over all frame hashes, spatial
anchor identifiers, and VLM annotation hashes within the window. This hash is
the input to the Solana raffle contract. When a reviewer marks a window as
\texttt{verified} and labels it \texttt{P}, the contract mints one raffle ticket
linked to the evidence hash, providing a cryptographic proof-of-work for
physical labor analogous to proof-of-stake for consensus. In the current
prototype, the transaction signature is computed locally as a deterministic hash
of worker ID and session seed, pending devnet deployment of the SPL transfer
contract; the on-chain architecture is fully specified and the devnet integration
is in progress.

\section{Experiments}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

We design three experiments using our prototype pipeline. Experiments 1 and 3
use real data from our Ironsite prototype. Experiment 2 is a prospective study
design stated as a falsifiable hypothesis awaiting human-labeled ground truth.

\subsection{Experiment 1: CII Classification on Sampled Frames}
\label{sec:exp1}

\textbf{Setup.} We applied CII classification to frames sampled from
\texttt{01\_production\_masonry.mp4} in two passes: (a) an early-segment pass
on 22 uniformly sampled frames covering $t = 0$--44~s (clip-only baseline from
prior analysis), and (b) a full-video pass on 30 frames sampled at 42.5-second
intervals across the full 1,276-second video. Each frame was classified
independently into P, C, or NC using the CII taxonomy.
Both passes used \textit{claude-sonnet-4-6} as the underlying vision-language
classifier: pass~(a) invoked the model directly on early-segment frames, while
pass~(b) routed frames through the \texttt{/cii/summary} endpoint which also
backends to \textit{claude-sonnet-4-6}; the two passes therefore differ only in
temporal sampling strategy, not in classifier identity.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The contrast between the two passes reveals a
known construction site pattern: early video segments contain disproportionate
setup and staging activity (high C, low P), while the full video shows
sustained masonry production ($\sim$87\% P, 95\% CI [69, 96] (Clopper-Pearson exact, $\alpha$=0.05), mean confidence

4 NC frames (13.3\%) --- two due to classification errors (confidence 0.20)
and two showing no workers visible (confidence 0.99) --- represent genuine
productivity gaps. The key limitation is that the full-video classifier has no
spatial grounding: it cannot distinguish whether a Productive label corresponds
to actual material placement at a specific wall location or a deceptive
simulation of masonry motion. This motivates the spatial anchoring requirement.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Evidence Anchoring vs.\ Clip-Only Review}
\label{sec:exp2}

\textbf{Hypothesis.} Human reviewers presented with spatially-anchored evidence
(COLMAP pose visualization + VLM bounding-box overlay + source clip) will
achieve higher inter-rater agreement and lower false-positive reward rate than
reviewers presented with source clip only.

\textbf{Proposed Study Design.} We plan to select 40 temporal windows from a
stratified sample of Ironsite videos, covering all three CII classes in
approximately equal proportion. Two independent reviewers will label each window
under two conditions: (a) \textbf{Clip-only}: source clip + CII taxonomy; (b)
\textbf{Anchored}: clip + COLMAP pose map + VLM annotations + adversarial flag
list.

\textbf{Primary metric.} Cohen's $\kappa$ inter-rater agreement, separately
computed for each condition. We predict $\kappa_{\text{anchored}} >
\kappa_{\text{clip-only}}$ by at least 0.15 ($p < 0.05$ under permutation test).

\textbf{Secondary metric.} False positive reward rate (windows labeled P that a
domain-expert adjudicator subsequently marks C or NC). We predict
$\text{FPR}_{\text{anchored}} < \text{FPR}_{\text{clip-only}}$.

\textbf{Falsifiability.} If $\kappa_{\text{anchored}} \leq \kappa_{\text{clip-only}}$,
the anchoring overhead is not justified and the simpler clip-only workflow should
be used. This experiment is the critical gate before production deployment.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Gaming Resistance Under Spatial Anchoring}
\label{sec:exp3}

\textbf{Setup.} We analyze the adversarial attack surface of clip-only versus
anchored reward systems under a game-theoretic model of a motivated worker
attempting to maximize reward tokens without performing productive work.
Figure~\ref{fig:gaming_sim} illustrates reward trajectory divergence between
honest and colluding worker strategies under this model.

\caption{Adversarial gaming simulation: reward trajectory comparison under

inflation by motivated adversaries; spatial anchoring under VINNA suppresses
the colluding trajectory by requiring COLMAP registration success.}
\label{fig:gaming_sim}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Clip-only attack surface.} A clip-only classifier
$f: \text{clip} \to \{P, C, NC\}$ has a fixed decision boundary in clip
feature space. An adversary can optimize:
\begin{equation}
  \delta^* = \arg\max_{\|\delta\| \leq \varepsilon}
             \Pr\bigl[f(x + \delta) = P\bigr]
\end{equation}
In the physical world, $\delta$ corresponds to camera motion patterns, object
arrangement, or proximity to reference surfaces. A construction worker who has
observed classifier outputs can learn the visual patterns that trigger $P$ labels
within days.

\textbf{Spatial anchor constraint.} Under VINNA, the attacker must
simultaneously satisfy:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item Produce frames the classifier labels P (satisfy $R_{\text{bin}}(f) = 1$).
\item Ensure COLMAP registers the frames into a consistent 3D reconstruction
  (requires stable, textured surfaces with sufficient keypoint matches, so
  $S(k) > 0$).
\item Ensure registered camera poses are consistent with the actual work location
  (spatial anchor must reference the correct jobsite region).
\item Ensure VLM annotations reference appropriate construction objects.
\end{enumerate}

Condition (2) is particularly constraining. In our prototype, 12 of 31 sampled
frames (38.7\%) failed COLMAP registration, primarily due to camera motion blur
and featureless surfaces. Performing real work naturally produces the stable,
textured, well-lit conditions needed for successful registration; deceptive
simulations are more likely to produce motion blur or inconsistent texture.

\textbf{Quantitative bound.} Let $p_{\text{real}}$ be the probability that a
window of real productive work passes COLMAP registration, and $p_{\text{fake}}$
be the probability that a deceptive window passes. In our prototype,
$p_{\text{real}} = 19/31 \approx 0.61$. We hypothesize $p_{\text{fake}} \ll
p_{\text{real}}$ because deceptive activity is unlikely to reproduce the dense
keypoint matches of actual tool-on-material contact. The expected reward gain
from gaming is proportional to $p_{\text{fake}} / p_{\text{real}}$, which
approaches zero in the limit of strict registration requirements.

\subsection{Preliminary Zone Attribution from COLMAP Poses}
\label{sec:zone-attr}

To demonstrate that spatially-anchored indexing can support a limited form of
spatial attribution, we applied temporal segmentation to partition the 30
sampled frames into three contiguous activity windows: Zone~A (Near Equipment,
frames 0--9, $n=10$), Zone~B (Scaffold, frames 10--19, $n=10$), and
Zone~C (Material Staging, frames 20--29, $n=10$). Zone boundaries are defined
by equal frame-index partitioning, not by camera-pose geometry; the zone labels
(Near Equipment, Scaffold, Material Staging) are assigned manually based on
visual inspection of representative frames from each temporal segment. Productive
(P) frames attributed to each zone were then counted from the CII classifier
output. This yields prototype outputs of the form: \emph{``Worker W003 had 43\%
of wrench-contact frames in Zone~A (Near Equipment)''} --- a coarse spatial
attribution that goes beyond per-frame classification.

We emphasize that this is preliminary: zone boundaries are derived from temporal
frame-index partitioning, not from camera-pose clustering or a semantic map of
the jobsite. The production path replaces this bucketing with XZ-plane K-means
($K=3$) over COLMAP-registered camera translation vectors, which would yield
geometrically grounded zone boundaries rather than uniform temporal windows.
This result demonstrates the \emph{path} from spatially-anchored indexing toward
zone-level spatial attribution; it does not constitute full spatial reasoning
over worker locations, zone coverage, or spatial state over time.

\subsection{Reward Score Illustration (SNRA)}

We illustrate the SNRA formula at two granularities.

\textbf{Per-window example (operational).}
In VINNA's deployed configuration, $R_{\text{SNRA}}$ is computed per 30-second
window.  For a representative window containing 2 productive frames
($R_{\text{bin}} = 1$) and spatial anchor confidence $S = 0.613$:
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{SNRA}} = \sigma(2) \cdot 0.613 \approx 0.880 \times 0.613 \approx 0.540
\end{equation}
This is the operationally meaningful score: the sigmoid is well inside the
linear regime ($\sigma(2) \approx 0.88$), so per-window reward scales
informatively with productive-frame count rather than saturating.

\textbf{Full-session aggregate (demonstration only).}
Aggregating all 30 sampled frames across the full 1,276-second video, 26 are
labeled P, yielding $\sum_{f} R_{\text{bin}}(f) = 26$.  This aggregate
\emph{should not be used as a single reward window} --- at this scale the
sigmoid trivially saturates ($\sigma(26) \approx 1.000$) and discriminability
is lost.  Shown here purely to illustrate the ledger totals:
\begin{equation}
  R_{\text{SNRA}}^{\text{aggregate}} = \sigma(26) \cdot 0.613
    \approx 1.000 \times 0.613 = 0.613
\end{equation}
Both scores would remain blocked pending human GT confirmation. The per-window
formulation is the intended deployment mode; the $\Delta t = 30$~s window
granularity prevents sigmoid saturation and preserves reward signal across
varying productivity levels.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Path from spatial indexing to spatial reasoning.} VINNA performs spatial
\emph{indexing} --- associating CII labels with camera-pose-registered frames
--- rather than full spatial reasoning over worker locations and zone states.
The zone attribution in \S\ref{sec:zone-attr} is a deliberate first step:
once COLMAP pose coverage exceeds 80\%, replacing temporal frame-index
partitioning with XZ-plane K-means over registered translation vectors yields
geometrically grounded zone boundaries and enables queries of the form
``Was worker W003 at the north wall or the scaffold during window w004?'' ---
the production path is architecturally complete and awaits denser reconstruction.

\textbf{Frame selection for full reconstruction.} The current 61.3\%
COLMAP registration rate is the primary bottleneck for coverage.
Applying sharpness-based pre-filtering and inter-frame optical-flow thresholding
before SfM submission is projected to raise registration above 80\%;
ORB-SLAM3~\cite{orbslam3_2021} replaces batch SfM with incremental pose tracking
for continuous long-video coverage without reprocessing cost.

\textbf{Human-study execution (highest-priority).} Experiment 2 is a prospective
design with a pre-registered falsifiable hypothesis
($\kappa_{\text{anchored}} - \kappa_{\text{clip-only}} \geq 0.15$, $p < 0.05$
permutation test). This is the critical gate before production deployment;
conducting the 40-window study with two independent CII-trained reviewers is the
next immediate step after hackathon.

\textbf{Contiguous video coverage.} The prototype samples 31 frames from a
1,276-second video (4.9\% temporal coverage). Full contiguous coverage is
achievable by parallelizing COLMAP across non-overlapping 60-second segments with
shared keyframe overlap for pose graph stitching.

\textbf{Empirical $p_{\text{fake}}$ measurement.} The gaming resistance argument
in Experiment~3 is currently theoretical. Constructing a 10--20 clip adversarial
test set of workers mimicking productive activity --- and measuring the COLMAP
registration rate of deceptive clips vs.\ genuine work --- would convert the
gaming-resistance bound from a hypothesis to a measured result.

\textbf{Legal and consent framework.} Worker video is sensitive biometric data.
Deployment requires explicit informed consent, data retention aligned with CCPA
and BIPA, and worker read-access to their own evidence records. The evidence
ledger's per-window hash structure is specifically designed to support
selective disclosure for worker-facing audits.

\textbf{Gaussian splatting integration.} The prototype generates a
COLMAP-compatible dataset for 3D Gaussian Splatting~\cite{gaussiansplatting};
completing the training pass (blocked by CUDA at hackathon time) would produce
a photorealistic spatial context view that significantly reduces reviewer
cognitive load during contested audits.

\textbf{Solana devnet deployment.} The current \texttt{tx\_sig} field is a
deterministic development mock (hash of worker ID and session seed); the
full SPL contract architecture is specified in the Devpost submission and is
deployable to devnet once the human-study gate (Experiment 2) confirms spatial
anchoring adds reviewer value --- ensuring the on-chain payout rail is
gated by validated evidence, not just implemented.

%%-------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
%%-------------------------------------------------------------------

The bottleneck for AI-driven construction safety incentives is not classification
accuracy --- it is evidence architecture. A P/C/NC classifier that outputs a label
without a replayable, tamper-evident evidence chain cannot support a credible
reward system: it has no defense against gaming and no mechanism for contested
audits. These are not implementation details --- they are the conditions under
which any real-money incentive system must operate.

VINNA builds this evidence architecture from commodity open-source tools: COLMAP
for spatially-anchored indexing, Qwen2.5-VL for semantic annotation, and a
JSON-based cryptographic ledger for integrity. The formal reward calculus ---
$R_{\text{bin}}(f) \in \{0,1\}$ for OSHA-aligned per-frame contribution and
$R_{\text{SNRA}}(w_k) = \sigma\!\bigl(\sum_f R_{\text{bin}}(f)\bigr) \cdot S(k)$
for spatially-weighted worker-level reward --- provides a mathematically precise
foundation deployable over any classifier, from a simple motion heuristic to
a state-of-the-art video foundation model.

The core claim holds unconditionally: any construction safety reward system
that issues real financial incentives must bind those incentives to spatial
evidence, not clip-level classification. The spatial anchor is what makes
the reward contestable, auditable, and resistant to the gaming incentives
that financial rewards inevitably create.
